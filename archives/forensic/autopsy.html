Medicolegal Autopsy: Core Concepts

  • Synonyms: Also called as necropsy or postmortem examination[span_9](start_span)[span_9](end_span).
  • Autopsy: Means (autos = self, opis = view) to see for oneself[span_10](start_span)[span_10](end_span).
  • Necropsy: (necros = dead, opis = view) is the most accurate term for the investigative dissection of the dead body, but the term autopsy is commonly used and is more popular[span_11](start_span)[span_11](end_span).

Types of Autopsy

  1. Clinical autopsy (pathological autopsy or academic autopsy)[span_12](start_span)[span_12](end_span)
  2. Medicolegal autopsy (forensic autopsy)[span_13](start_span)[span_13](end_span)
  3. Anatomical autopsy[span_14](start_span)[span_14](end_span)
Negative autopsy definition: Cause of death remains unknown even after thorough history, examination, toxicological investigation[span_15](start_span)[span_15](end_span).

1. Clinical Autopsy

  • It is done by a Medical Practitioner or treating doctor with the consent of relatives to know the diagnosis or to confirm the diagnosis[span_16](start_span)[span_16](end_span).
  • Here the autopsy may be complete or incomplete (partial) depending upon the consent obtained for that part of body[span_17](start_span)[span_17](end_span).
  • It is not done under legal obligation, therefore no requisition from police is required[span_18](start_span)[span_18](end_span).
  • For doing clinical autopsy, consent of relatives is a must. Without consent, a doctor cannot proceed for clinical autopsy[span_19](start_span)[span_19](end_span).

2. Medicolegal Autopsy

  • Medicolegal autopsy is a scientific examination of a dead body. It is carried out under the laws of State only on the requisition of a legal authority responsible for the investigation of sudden, suspicious or unnatural death[span_20](start_span)[span_20](end_span).
  • The legal authority is usually a police officer not below the rank of sub-inspector or an Executive Magistrate[span_21](start_span)[span_21](end_span).
  • Medicolegal autopsy is also called as forensic autopsy. But the popular term for layperson remains postmortem examination[span_22](start_span)[span_22](end_span).

Objectives of Autopsy

  • To make positive identification of the body[span_39](start_span)[span_39](end_span).
  • To determine the cause of death[span_40](start_span)[span_40](end_span).
  • To determine the mode of dying and time of death, where necessary and possible[span_41](start_span)[span_41](end_span).
  • To demonstrate all the external and internal abnormalities, malformation and diseases[span_42](start_span)[span_42](end_span).
  • To detect, describe and measure any external and internal injuries[span_43](start_span)[span_43](end_span).
  • To obtain samples for analysis, histological, microbiological, toxicological and other necessary investigations[span_44](start_span)[span_44](end_span).
  • To retain foreign bodies, organs or tissue as evidence[span_45](start_span)[span_45](end_span).
  • To obtain photographs, video and films for evidence[span_46](start_span)[span_46](end_span).
  • To provide full written report of the autopsy findings and to make an expert opinion of those findings[span_47](start_span)[span_47](end_span).
  • To restore the body to the best possible cosmetic condition before release to the relatives[span_48](start_span)[span_48](end_span).

Rules for Medicolegal Autopies in Nepal

  • Body should be labeled as soon as it arrives in the mortuary[span_49](start_span)[span_49](end_span).
  • Conducted only when there is an official written order from police or magistrate (CDO in Nepal)[span_50](start_span)[span_50](end_span).
  • Conducted only in authorised government hospital[span_51](start_span)[span_51](end_span).
  • Conducted by a registered medical practitioner with a training in forensic medicine[span_52](start_span)[span_52](end_span).
  • Conducted in a mortuary and never in a private room[span_53](start_span)[span_53](end_span).
  • Medical officer should first read the inquest report to find out the apparent cause of death. He can also know about significant points he has to focus on. For eg: To collect samples for toxicological analysis in poisoning, microbiological study etc; if a drainage tube was inserted it might be confused with a stab wound[span_54](start_span)[span_54](end_span).
  • Examine examination in daylight as far as possible[span_55](start_span)[span_55](end_span).
  • If received in the evening, preliminary examination is done to note external appearances, body temperature, rigor mortis, postmortem lividity etc. and autopsy is performed next morning[span_56](start_span)[span_56](end_span).
  • No unauthorized person should be present at autopsy[span_57](start_span)[span_57](end_span).
  • Inform the investigating officer if any dissimilarity is found in the injuries actually found on examination and that noted in the inquest report[span_58](start_span)[span_58](end_span).
  • The details should be noted by an assistant while the medical officer is performing autopsy[span_59](start_span)[span_59](end_span).
  • Nothing should be erased or anything altered should be initialed[span_60](start_span)[span_60](end_span).
  • Both positive and negative findings should be noted[span_61](start_span)[span_61](end_span).
  • Body should be handed over to the police after completing autopsy[span_62](start_span)[span_62](end_span).
  • Postmortem report should not be issued to the party[span_63](start_span)[span_63](end_span).
  • In newborn infants, to determine the question of livebirth and viability[span_64](start_span)[span_64](end_span).

Autopsy Protocol Structure

A protocol is a signed document containing a written record which serves as proof of something. It is used in two basic forms: Narrative (in story form) and Numerical (in numbers)[span_65](start_span)[span_65](end_span).

  • Preamble: Mention about authority ordering examination, time of arrival of body at mortuary, date and place of examination, name, age and sex of the deceased and means by which the body was identified[span_66](start_span)[span_66](end_span).
  • Body of report: Complete description of external and internal examination of the body[span_67](start_span)[span_67](end_span).
  • Conclusion: Conclusion of the cause of death[span_68](start_span)[span_68](end_span).

Clothing, Scrapings, and Swabs Protocol

  • Clothing: Should be listed, examined and described. Should not be thrown on the ground. Wet clothes should be air dried. Stains should be kept for analysis. Clean plastic bags should be used for packing[span_94](start_span)[span_94](end_span).
  • Nail Scrapings: Visible fibres or other matter should be removed. Ten small envelopes are labeled, one for each finger[span_95](start_span)[span_95](end_span).
  • Vaginal and Anal Swabs: Swabs from suspected areas of seminal staining are taken. Pubic hair should be combed through. Matted pubic hair should be cut out with scissors and samples of pubic hair taken[span_96](start_span)[span_96](end_span).

5. Postmortem Changes to Log

  • Postmortem lividity (position, colour, blanching or not)[span_97](start_span)[span_97](end_span).
  • Rigor mortis[span_98](start_span)[span_98](end_span).
  • Signs of decomposition (any greenish discolouration, marbling etc.)[span_99](start_span)[span_99](end_span)

6. Documentation of Injuries

  • Explain any ligature mark on neck[span_100](start_span)[span_100](end_span).
  • External wounds - in detail noting the site, size, approximate distance from midline or nearest bony prominence, type (abrasion, contusion, incision etc), possible weapon causing the wound (sharp or blunt)[span_101](start_span)[span_101](end_span).
  • Surgical procedures, applied dressings and other diagnostic and therapeutic measures found on the body[span_102](start_span)[span_102](end_span).

Significance of Each Incision Type

  1. I-shaped incision: It is an easy and quicker type of incision. Most commonly preferred type of incision[span_119](start_span)[span_119](end_span).
  2. Y-shaped incision: In many religions, e.g. Christians, the dead body is kept in a coffin during funeral, during which many of the relatives would be watching the dead body, especially the face and anterior chest wall, which are usually exposed. So it is preferable to preserve cosmetic appearance of these body parts as good as possible. With the Y-incision, breasts and chest wall are spared, so this type of incision is better in these cases[span_120](start_span)[span_120](end_span).
  3. Modified Y-shaped incision: It is done in cases of hanging and strangulation. In these cases, neck dissection findings are very important in differentiating whether it is hanging or strangulation. For this, neck vessels should be drained empty so that blood would not cause confusion in interpretation of findings during neck dissection. This is made possible with the modified Y-incision that allows bloodless neck dissection[span_121](start_span)[span_121](end_span).

Internal Examination (All Three Cavities)

a. Cranial Cavity [2067, 66]

  • Head position: wooden block placed under shoulder to extend neck, and head is fixed by head rest which should have semicircular groove to hold back of neck[span_122](start_span)[span_122](end_span).
  • Scalp incision: Coronal incision is made, which starts from mastoid process just behind the ear and is carried over vertex of scalp to back of opposite ear (intermastoidal incision)[span_123](start_span)[span_123](end_span). Scalp reflected forwards to suprayciliary ridges and backwards to point just below occipital protuberance[span_124](start_span)[span_124](end_span). Record any contusion and fracture of skull bones[span_125](start_span)[span_125](end_span).
  • Removal of skull cap: Saw line made slightly in V-shaped direction so that the skull cap can fit back into correct position on reconstruction of body[span_126](start_span)[span_126](end_span).
  • Dura and Sinus Screening: Examine the brain with intact dura. Note should be made for congestion of meninges, extradural or subdural hemorrhages[span_127](start_span)[span_127](end_span). Superior longitudinal sinus is opened along its length with scalpel and carefully examined for antemortem thrombus[span_128](start_span)[span_128](end_span).
  • Removal of duramater: Duramater is grasped anteriorly with forceps, and with the help of scalpel it is cut all along the line of cleavage of skull and fold back to midline[span_129](start_span)[span_129](end_span). Flax cerebri is removed from cribiform palate and dura, and flax are pulled back[span_130](start_span)[span_130](end_span). Examine the surface of brain for subdural and subarachnoid hemorrhage[span_131](start_span)[span_131](end_span).
  • Brain Extraction: Insert four fingers of left hand between frontal lobes and skull[span_132](start_span)[span_132](end_span). Draw the lobes backwards gently and cut optic nerves and vessels as they emerge from skull[span_133](start_span)[span_133](end_span). Cut the tentorium cerebelli along the superior border of petrous bone and along its attachments in posterior cranial fossa[span_134](start_span)[span_134](end_span). Cut spinal cord, first cervical nerves and vertebral arteries as low as possible through foramen magnum[span_135](start_span)[span_135](end_span). Support the brain in left hand and remove with cerebellum[span_136](start_span)[span_136](end_span). Remove the pituitary by chiseling the posterior clinoid process and incising the diaphragm of sella tursica around its periphery[span_137](start_span)[span_137](end_span). Surface of brain and base are examined for hemorrhage, injury or disease[span_138](start_span)[span_138](end_span). Conditions of vessels especially circle of Willis is noted for presence of arteriosclerosis, minute aneurysms, etc[span_139](start_span)[span_139](end_span).

Dissection of Brain

  • Brain is placed in anatomical position and sliced into two halves by a long knife cutting through corpus callosum and through midline of midbrain, pons, medulla[span_140](start_span)[span_140](end_span).
  • Cut the brain in serial sections 1 cm apart, beginning at frontal lobe and passing backward to the occiput[span_141](start_span)[span_141](end_span).
  • Cerebellum: Cut through vermis to expose 4th ventricle. Oblique incision made through each hemisphere to expose the dentate nucleus[span_142](start_span)[span_142](end_span).

b. Spinal Cord

Examination is not routinely done. If it is necessary, it is examined from back[span_143](start_span)[span_143](end_span):

  • Midline incision is given at back along the entire length of neck and trunk, skin reflected cut on either side laterally for about 2.5 cms[span_144](start_span)[span_144](end_span).
  • Vertical column is chiseled out along the medial margins of the transverse processes of vertebrae. Electric saw may also be used in cutting[span_145](start_span)[span_145](end_span).
  • Entire length of the spinal cord can be taken out without causing postmortem trauma[span_146](start_span)[span_146](end_span).

Note: If special neck dissection is to be done as in hanging, ligature strangulation, neck dissection is done after cranial, thoracic, abdominal dissection as it creates a dry field[span_147](start_span)[span_147](end_span).

Methods of Removal of Organs

  1. Virchow's Technique: Individual organs are removed one by one. Cranial cavity is exposed first, followed by thoracic, cervical and abdominal organs. Spinal cord is removed from back. Here, anatomico-pathological relation is not preserved[span_157](start_span)[span_157](end_span).
  2. Rokitansky's Technique: In-situ dissection is done. Then en-block removal is done. Preferred in highly transmissible diseases like HIV, Hepatitis B etc. Note: Rokitansky method is the preferred technique in infants[span_158](start_span)[span_158](end_span).
  3. Lettulle's Technique: Cervical, thoracic, abdominal and pelvic organs are removed en masse. Then dissect as organ block. All attachments are intact[span_159](start_span)[span_159](end_span).
  4. Ghon's Technique: Cervical, thoracic, abdominal organs and urogenital system are removed as organ block. Neuronal system is removed as another block[span_160](start_span)[span_160](end_span).

Postmortem Thrombi Morphologies

  • Red currant jelly: Blood clots rapidly, producing a soft, lumpy, uniformly dark-red, slippery, moist clot[span_184](start_span)[span_184](end_span).
  • Chicken fat: When red cells sediment before blood coagulates, a clot similar to red currant is formed at the bottom, above which a bright yellow layer of serum and fibrin is seen[span_185](start_span)[span_185](end_span).

2. Respiratory System

  • Trachea: Incised longitudinally. See if it is intact and note the content if any[span_186](start_span)[span_186](end_span).
  • Lungs: Note the pleural surface. Normally it is smooth and glistening. Note the feel. Normally it is spongy in consistency. On sectioning, note the appearance. Normally it is congested[span_187](start_span)[span_187](end_span).

3. Gastrointestinal System

  • Oesophagus: Incise longitudinally. Note the content[span_188](start_span)[span_188](end_span).
  • Stomach: Remove after applying ligature to both ends. Open it along greater curvature from cardiac to pyloric end. Note the content and its volume[span_189](start_span)[span_189](end_span).
  • Intestine: Note the serosa, diameter of various portions, colour of wall, adhesions, content and its volume[span_190](start_span)[span_190](end_span).

4. Hepatobiliary, Urogenital, & Musculoskeletal

  • Liver & Pancreas: Note the colour. Note if it is grossly intact. After sectioning, note if it is friable. Pancreas is also examined[span_191](start_span)[span_191](end_span).

05. Applied Case Study: Obstructed Labor Demise [2070/11]

MATERNAL FINDINGS (Mother)
• External: Signs of severe exhaustion/dehydration, dry mucous membranes, marked vulval and vaginal edema, bruising/lacerations of the birth canal. Bandl’s ring (pathological retraction ring) may be visible across the lower abdomen[span_201](start_span)[span_201](end_span).
• Internal: Uterine rupture (usually lower segment) with extensive intraperitoneal hemorrhage, or massive ischemic necrosis of the uterine wall. The fetal head may be wedged tightly into the pelvic brim[span_202](start_span)[span_202](end_span).
• Cause of Death: Hemorrhagic shock (secondary to uterine rupture) OR Septic shock (secondary to prolonged infection/chorioamnionitis)[span_203](start_span)[span_203](end_span).
• Manner of Death: Natural[span_204](start_span)[span_204](end_span).
• Mechanism of Death: Acute cardiovascular collapse / Multiorgan dysfunction syndrome (MODS)[span_205](start_span)[span_205](end_span).
FETAL FINDINGS (Baby)
• External: Extreme distortion and moulding of the skull bones. Massive caput succedaneum or extensive cephalhematoma. Peeling skin or maceration changes depending on time elapsed since intrauterine death[span_206](start_span)[span_206](end_span).
• Internal: Intracranial hemorrhage (subdural/subarachnoid) secondary to severe bone overriding. Asphyxial signs: widespread visceral petechiae (Tardieu spots on pleura/pericardium), thick meconium aspiration in the trachea and major bronchi[span_207](start_span)[span_207](end_span).

06. Collection of Viscera in Suspected Poisoning

  1. Blood: At least 10 ml collected in a bottle, commonly from subclavian vessels or femoral artery[span_208](start_span)[span_208](end_span). For alcohol cases, blood not collected from heart, pleura or abdominal cavities as it may give higher values[span_209](start_span)[span_209](end_span). No preservative required, if blood is only for grouping[span_210](start_span)[span_210](end_span). Blood may be alternatively absorbed on a white filter paper or clean sterile cloth and dried[span_211](start_span)[span_211](end_span).
  2. CSF: Obtained by: Cisternal puncture, Aspiration with Pasteur pipette from base of brain, or Puncture of lateral ventricles. Injected drug takes 30 min to reach CSF in a living person[span_212](start_span)[span_212](end_span).
  3. Bone: About 200 gm is collected. 10-15 cm of shaft of femur can be collected[span_213](start_span)[span_213](end_span).
  4. Bone Marrow: Obtained from femur, sternum or vertebra[span_214](start_span)[span_214](end_span).
  5. Hair: Adequate sample of head and pubic hair, plucked out complete with root, by artery forceps[span_215](start_span)[span_215](end_span).
  6. Muscle: Wedge of thigh muscle (quadriceps)[span_216](start_span)[span_216](end_span).
  7. Nail: All the finger and toe nails are removed entirely from their bed[span_217](start_span)[span_217](end_span).
  8. Skin: A piece of at least 2.5 sq. cm from affected area in corrosive poisoning. From thigh or back in heavy metal poisoning. If needle puncture is seen, whole needle track and surrounding tissue is removed. Control is taken from opposite side and kept in separate container[span_218](start_span)[span_218](end_span).
  9. Urine: Collected by suprapubic puncture or opening the bladder[span_219](start_span)[span_219](end_span).
  10. Vitreous: Collected by fine hypodermic needle attached to a 5 ml syringe. Collected from outer canthus of eye[span_220](start_span)[span_220](end_span).
  11. Stomach, Duodenum and Pancreas: Removed together by applying double ligatures[span_221](start_span)[span_221](end_span).
  12. Liver: Remove separately or attached to stomach and duodenum[span_222](start_span)[span_222](end_span).
  13. Spleen: Removed by cutting through the pedicle[span_223](start_span)[span_223](end_span).

Preservation of Viscera Mandates & Quantities

Viscera should be preserved in: Death suspected to be due to poisoning either by police or the doctor; Deceased was intoxicated or used drugs; Cause of death not found after autopsy; When an unusual smell, colour or an unidentifiable material is detected in the stomach; Anaphylactic deaths; Death due to burns; Advanced decomposition; Accidental death involving driver of a vehicle or machine operator[span_235](start_span)[span_235](end_span).

  • Stomach and its contents: Whole[span_236](start_span)[span_236](end_span)
  • Small intestine: 30 cm (entire in infants)[span_237](start_span)[span_237](end_span)
  • Small intestine contents: 100 gm[span_238](start_span)[span_238](end_span)
  • Liver: Portion involving gall bladder (500 gm)[span_239](start_span)[span_239](end_span)
  • Kidney: Each half[span_240](start_span)[span_240](end_span)
  • Blood: 30 ml, minimum 10 ml[span_241](start_span)[span_241](end_span)
  • Urine: 30 ml[span_242](start_span)[span_242](end_span)
  • Heart: Preserved for Strychnine, digitalis, yellow oleander cases[span_243](start_span)[span_243](end_span)

Preservatives Choice Framework

  • Saturated sodium chloride solution: Used except in poisoning from corrosive acids, alkalis, corrosive sublimate and aconite[span_244](start_span)[span_244](end_span).
  • Rectified spirit: Used except in cases of: Alcohol, kerosene, Chloroform, ether, Chloral hydrate, Formic acid, Formaldehyde, acetic acid, Phenol, Phosphorus, Paraldehyde[span_245](start_span)[span_245](end_span).
  • Blood Preservation: 10 mg/ml of sodium or potassium fluoride and 3 mg of potassium oxalate. Fluoride should be added to urine, CSF and vitreous humour for analysis of Alcohol, Cocaine, Cyanide, CO[span_246](start_span)[span_246](end_span).
  • Oxalic acid, Ethylene glycol, Fluoride, CO in blood: 30 mg sodium citrate added to 10 ml of blood[span_247](start_span)[span_247](end_span).
  • Urine alternate stabilization: 1 ml of conc. HCl or 100 mg of thymol or 100 mg of NaF for 10 ml of urine[span_248](start_span)[span_248](end_span).

Note: Preservatives not necessary if viscera can be examined within 24 hrs, sample can be kept in refrigerator, or for bone, hair, nail, and lung (for detecting inhaled poisons). Viscera should not be preserved in formaldehyde. Use glass bottles with wide mouth; avoid rubber inserts (extracts chloroform/phenols) and plastics (volatile poisons diffuse through)[span_249](start_span)[span_249](end_span).

Instruction for Preservation and Dispatch of the Viscera

  • Stomach and its contents, small intestine and its contents are preserved in one bottle, and liver and kidney in another bottle. Blood and urine are preserved separately[span_250](start_span)[span_250](end_span).
  • Stomach and intestines are opened before they are preserved[span_251](start_span)[span_251](end_span).
  • Liver and kidney are cut to 0.5 to 1 cm pieces or minced in a grinder or mixer[span_252](start_span)[span_252](end_span).
  • Quantity of preservative and viscera should be equal in bulk[span_253](start_span)[span_253](end_span).
  • Only two-thirds of the capacity of the bottle should be filled with viscera and preservative, to avoid bursting of bottle if gases of decomposition are formed[span_254](start_span)[span_254](end_span).
  • Stoppers should be tightly fitted[span_255](start_span)[span_255](end_span).
  • Label the bottles properly with the name of the victim, age, sex, autopsy number, police station, crime number, organ it contains, date and place of autopsy, preservative used and signature[span_256](start_span)[span_256](end_span).
  • Sample of preservative used, i.e. 25 ml of rectified spirit or 25 gm of sodium chloride, is separately kept and sent for analysis[span_257](start_span)[span_257](end_span).
  • Sealed bottles are put into a box that is locked and the lock is sealed[span_258](start_span)[span_258](end_span).
  • A copy of the inquest report, postmortem report and authorization letter are sent to the "Regional Forensic Science Laboratory" (RFSL) along with the viscera[span_259](start_span)[span_259](end_span).
  • Key of the box and the sample seal on a piece of paper are kept in an envelope which is sealed and sent with viscera box[span_260](start_span)[span_260](end_span).
  • Viscera box is handed over to the police after taking a receipt, who delivers it personally to the RFSL[span_261](start_span)[span_261](end_span).

07. Biohazards, Skeletal Remains & Exhumation

AIDS and Autopsy Bio-Precautions

  • All infected bodies should be wrapped and tied in double layer tough plastic bag, with a red colour tag mentioning "Biologically hazardous". Label properly mentioning name, age, sex, registration number etc[span_262](start_span)[span_262](end_span).
  • Workers with exudative lesions or weeping dermatitis or external injury should not handle AIDS victims[span_263](start_span)[span_263](end_span).
  • Wear items checklist: Full sleeves, Waterproof plastic apron, Head cap, Face mask, Goggles if eye glasses are not worn, Double gloves (heavy autopsy gloves over surgical gloves), Waterproof rubber gumboots of knee length with shoe cover, A plastic visor, Particulate air-filled respirator[span_264](start_span)[span_264](end_span).
  • Avoid accidental pricks and cuts from sharps. In case of cut, remove the gloves immediately and replace. Wash exposed skin surface thoroughly[span_265](start_span)[span_265](end_span).
  • Avoid mucocutaneous and aerosol contact with the body fluids. Handle specimen with gloved hands, place it in 10% formalin and label properly[span_266](start_span)[span_266](end_span).
  • Dip instruments in 20% glutaraldehyde for half-an-hour, wash with soap or detergent and water, dry, rinse in methylated spirit and air dry[span_267](start_span)[span_267](end_span).
  • Laundry material (apron, towel etc) should be soaked in 1% bleach for half-an-hour, washed with detergent and hot water and autoclaved[span_268](start_span)[span_268](end_span).
  • Autopsy table and floor should be cleaned with 1% bleach solution, followed by washing with soap and water. Use 1:10 dilution of common household bleach or freshly prepared sodium hypochlorite solution as disinfectant[span_269](start_span)[span_269](end_span).
  • Accidental injuries should be washed under running water and bleeding encouraged and disinfected[span_270](start_span)[span_270](end_span).
  • After autopsy, all body should be packed and the body should be wrapped in double layer heavy plastic sheet bag and secured properly. Attach a tag for identification. Wash hands and face with soap and water and rinse in 70% methylated spirit. Body should be burnt or incinerated[span_271](start_span)[span_271](end_span).

Note: Universal precaution does not apply to faeces, nasal secretions, sputum, sweat, tears, urine and vomitus until they contain visible blood[span_272](start_span)[span_272](end_span).

Examination of Mutilated Bodies or Fragments

Mutilated bodies are those which are extensively disfigured or in which a limb or a part is lost, but the soft tissues, muscles and skin are attached to the bone[span_273](start_span)[span_273](end_span). First photograph the scene before disturbing anything. Arrange the body parts in anatomic order. Send appropriate specimen for toxicological analysis, DNA profiling, such as muscle, piece of long bone, plucked hair[span_274](start_span)[span_274](end_span). Note the following:

  • Human or animal: By shape, structure, weight of objects. Or performing precipitin test or anti-globulin inhibition test using blood or other soft tissue may help[span_275](start_span)[span_275](end_span).
  • One or more bodies: Fit together the separated parts. If there is no duplication, colour is uniform, then it belongs to same body[span_276](start_span)[span_276](end_span).
  • Sex: Can be differentiated if head or trunk is available, from the presence and distribution of hair, character of pelvis, skull etc[span_277](start_span)[span_277](end_span).
  • Age: Can be found out from general development, skull, teeth and ossification of bones[span_278](start_span)[span_278](end_span).
  • Stature: Can be determined from measurement of long bones[span_279](start_span)[span_279](end_span).
  • Identity: Determined from fingerprints, tattoo marks, scars, moles, hair, teeth etc[span_280](start_span)[span_280](end_span).
  • Manner of separation of parts: Examine margins of parts whether cut, sawn, hacked, lacerated or disarticulated[span_281](start_span)[span_281](end_span).
  • Time since death: May be determined from condition of the parts[span_282](start_span)[span_282](end_span).
  • Cause of death (Antemortem or postmortem): Examine the margin of parts for evidence of vital reaction[span_283](start_span)[span_283](end_span).

Virtual Autopsy (Virtopsy) & Psychological Autopsy

  • Virtual Autopsy (Virtopsy): Non-invasive technique of examining dead bodies to find the cause of death using a combination of CT and MR imaging. CT gives information about morbid anatomical findings, MR imaging focuses on soft tissues, and MR spectroscopy can measure metabolites formed due to decomposition. Emphysema, air embolism, pneumothorax, hyperbaric trauma and decompression effects are better appreciated. For eg: In a firearm injury, entrance and exit wounds can be determined on fracture pattern with inward and outward bevelling of bone. Track of projectile can be studied[span_284](start_span)[span_284](end_span).
  • Psychological Autopsy: It is a set of postmortem investigative procedures that help ascertain and evaluate the role that physical and psychological factors play in the death of the victim of suicide, thus to determine the manner of death to as high a degree of certainty as possible. Data are sought regarding: Medical history, Educational history, Sexual history, Marital history, Employment history, Alcohol/drugs use, Phobias, Moods, Interpersonal relationship, Friends and colleagues, Personality, Coping style, Psychiatric history, I.Q., and Chronology of events leading to suicide[span_285](start_span)[span_285](end_span).

Negative vs Obscure Autopsies Deep Dive [2071]

Negative Autopsy Reasons: 2-5% of all autopsies are negative[span_286](start_span)[span_286](end_span). Causes include:

  • Inadequate history: Cases such as death from vagal inhibition, status epilepticus, hypersensitivity reaction, laryngeal spasm in drowning etc. may not show any anatomical findings[span_287](start_span)[span_287](end_span).
  • Inadequate external examination: Findings missed such as fresh and old needle marks in a drug addict, snake/insect bites, or burns in electrocution[span_288](start_span)[span_288](end_span).
  • Inadequate internal examination: Findings missed such as air embolism, pneumothorax etc[span_289](start_span)[span_289](end_span).
  • Insufficient laboratory findings, lack of toxicological analysis, or lack of training of doctor[span_290](start_span)[span_290](end_span).

Obscure Autopsies Definition: Those autopsies which do not show a definite cause for death, in which there are minimal, indefinite or obscure findings, or even no positive findings at all[span_291](start_span)[span_291](end_span). Causes include:

  • Natural diseases: With obscure or some microscopic findings, death by emotion/work-stress, functional failure such as epilepsy, paroxysmal fibrillation etc[span_292](start_span)[span_292](end_span).
  • Biochemical disturbance: Uraemia, Diabetes, Potassium deficiency etc[span_293](start_span)[span_293](end_span).
  • Endocrine dysfunction: Adrenal insufficiency, Thyrotoxicosis or myxedema[span_294](start_span)[span_294](end_span).
  • Concealed trauma: Cerebral concussion, self-reduced neck injury, blunt injury to the heart, reflex vagal inhibition[span_295](start_span)[span_295](end_span).
  • Poisoning: Delayed suboxic or narcotic poisoning, anaesthetic overdosage, neurotoxic or cytotoxic poisons, plant poisons[span_296](start_span)[span_296](end_span).
  • Miscellaneous: Allergy, drug idiosyncrasy etc[span_297](start_span)[span_297](end_span).

Examination of Skeletal Remains (Forensic Anthropology)

  1. Are they actually bones?[span_298](start_span)[span_298](end_span)
  2. Human or Animal: By shape, structure, weight of objects. Or performing precipitin test, DNA analysis of bones[span_299](start_span)[span_299](end_span).
  3. One or More Individuals: By reconstructing the skeleton. The colour emitted when the surface of bones are irradiated with ultraviolet light helps to differentiate if the bones are from one or more individuals[span_300](start_span)[span_300](end_span).
  4. Sex: Examine pelvis, skull, femur, manubrium-gladiolus ratio, diameter of head of femur and humerus. Measurement of femur, tibia, humerus and radius aid in sex differentiation[span_301](start_span)[span_301](end_span).
  5. Age at Death: Examine teeth, ossific centres, amount of wear/tear in teeth, length of long bones, epiphyseal union, pubic symphysis, closure of skull sutures, cortical resorption, bony lipping, osteoporosis, calcification, osteoarthritic changes[span_302](start_span)[span_302](end_span).
  6. Race: Examine skull and face measurements, teeth, lower extremities[span_303](start_span)[span_303](end_span).
  7. Stature: Measure long bones by osteometric board. Formulas used: Pearson's formula, Trotter and Gleser formulae, Harrison and Dupertuid formulas. Error of up to 2.5 cm can occur. Humerus is 20%, tibia 22%, femur 27% and spine 35% of individual's height in life[span_304](start_span)[span_304](end_span).
  8. Identification: Teeth, disease/deformities of bone, fractures, orthopedic prosthesis, congenital defects, superimposition technique, previous x-rays, dental charts[span_305](start_span)[span_305](end_span).
  9. Nature of Injury: Examine end of long bones to see if its margin has been cut, sawn, hacked, disarticulated[span_306](start_span)[span_306](end_span).
  10. Time Since Death: Process of skeletonisation tracking: soft tissue disappears first, then articular cartilage, then ligaments[span_307](start_span)[span_307](end_span).
  11. Cause of Death: Look for evidence of fractures, injuries, firearm marks, bone disease, or metallic poisons[span_308](start_span)[span_308](end_span).

Exhumation Protocol

Definition: It is the retrieval of a previously buried body for postmortem examination. Exhumation is done from a legitimate grave, else it is a scene of crime[span_310](start_span)[span_310](end_span). Objectives: Investigation of civil-legal matter, further investigation in criminal case, ancient historical study, tracking cause/manner of death, method of disposal, or recording injuries[span_311](start_span)[span_311](end_span).

  • Done in: Criminal cases (Homicide, suspected homicide disguised as suicide, suspicious poisoning, death from criminal abortion/negligence); Civil cases (Accidental death claim, insurance, workmen compensation, professional negligence liability, inheritance or disputed identity)[span_312](start_span)[span_312](end_span).
  • Procedure: Authorization (No laws in Nepal). Antemortem data of the alleged person should be available. Proper identification of the grave. Necessary logistic support should be available. Start early on a dry day. Relevant authorities and personnel should be present (doctors, forensic anthropologist, archaeologist, fingerprint expert, forensic odontologist, ballistic expert etc). Photos and videos of the scene should be taken. Block visibility from public[span_313](start_span)[span_313](end_span).
  • Dissection Environment & Handling: Soil from above, below and two sides of the body or coffin should be collected in clean containers separately. Examine body inside the grave/coffin regarding position and appearance. Burial uncovered 10 to 15 cm at a time. Notes made about condition of soil, water content, vegetable growth. Proper collection, chain of custody, and transport to mortuary. Full autopsy performed in usual manner to prepare report[span_314](start_span)[span_314](end_span).
  • Antemortem Data Bank (AMD): Refers to all data during life of the individual believed to be buried there. Includes: Name, age, sex, address, religion, ethnicity, occupation, height, weight, built, obesity, wearing garments detailed when missing, health conditions/medications, personal habits (tobacco, alcohol, khaini, drug etc.), scars, hair style/dye, moustache/beard, tattoos, eye glasses, hearing aids, jewelry, piercings, specific congenital defects, dental data, and official/hospital records/X-rays. Note: Three scientific methods of identification are: Finger printing, Dental, and DNA analysis[span_315](start_span)[span_315](end_span).
  • Disappearance Pipeline: Antemortem data bank for all missing persons is collected → Organised and systematic investigation of all graves is done → Centralised data analysis is done → Proper and prompt DNA analysis from bones → Appropriate body handover procedures after identification[span_316](start_span)[span_316](end_span).

08. Artifacts (Important from Viva point of view)

Definition: It is any change or feature introduced in a body after death (accidental or physiologically unrelated finding to the natural state of the body), that is likely to lead to misinterpretation of medicolegally significant antemortem findings[span_317](start_span)[span_317](end_span).

Artifacts Introduced Between Death and Autopsy

  • Agonal Artifacts: Asphyxia causes vomiting as a result of air passage gets filled with vomit. It might be wrongly thought of as a cause of asphyxia, while in fact it is the result[span_318](start_span)[span_318](end_span). Oesophagogastromalacia: Seen in severe head injury. Occurs due to autodigestion; stomach contents get spilled and affected tissue turns grayish white to black[span_319](start_span)[span_319](end_span).
  • Resuscitation Defects: Injection marks of resuscitation, ring-like contusion due to defibrillator, external massage of the chest can cause contusion, ribs fracture[span_320](start_span)[span_320](end_span).
  • Artifacts Due to Handling of the Body: Rough handling can cause fractures. Fresh abrasions due to dragging of the body after death[span_321](start_span)[span_321](end_span).
  • Artifacts Related to Rigor Mortis: Rigor may break partially due to rough handling. Rigor affecting heart may simulate concentric hypertrophy of the heart. Atmospheric condition can alter rigor[span_322](start_span)[span_322](end_span).
  • Artifacts Related to Postmortem Lividity: Poisons can change colour of postmortem lividity. For eg: cherry red in CO poisoning, bright red in HCN etc[span_323](start_span)[span_323](end_span).
  • Artifacts Due to Burns: Heat ruptures may resemble lacerated or incised wounds. Heat haematoma simulate extradural haemorrhage[span_324](start_span)[span_324](end_span).
  • Artifacts in Firearm Wounds: Drainage wounds may be mistaken for firearm wounds. Track of projectile may be altered by embalming[span_325](start_span)[span_325](end_span).
  • Artifacts Due to Animal and Insect Bites (Anthrapophagy): Bites of dogs resemble stab wounds. Extensive linear ant lesions around neck resemble ligature abrasions[span_326](start_span)[span_326](end_span).
  • Artifacts in Brain: Rough handling can cause contusions and damage[span_327](start_span)[span_327](end_span).
  • Artifacts in Liver: Undersurface of the liver in contact with transverse colon shows greenish discoloration due to putrefaction[span_328](start_span)[span_328](end_span).
  • Postmortem Haemorrhage: Postmortem injury may damage blood vessel and cause haemorrhage[span_329](start_span)[span_329](end_span).
  • Artifacts Due to Decomposition: Postmortem lividity may be confused with bruising. Bloody fluid is found in the mouth, nose and ears as a result of decomposition. Subcutaneous emphysema occurs as a result of decomposition. Separation of sutures of skull can occur in infant brain due to gas collected as a result of decomposition[span_330](start_span)[span_330](end_span).
  • Toxicological Artifacts: Faulty sampling can give false results. Collection of blood from heart with long needles can cause mixing with stomach or oesophageal contents. Decomposition causes an increase in concentration of CO[span_331](start_span)[span_331](end_span).

Artifacts Introduced During Autopsy

  • Air in Blood Vessels: Pulling of dura in the sagittal line will cause the air to enter the blood vessel at the top of brain[span_332](start_span)[span_332](end_span).
  • Skull Fractures: Can occur due to forceful pull of the skull cap[span_333](start_span)[span_333](end_span).
  • Visceral Damage: Rough handling can cause damage to the brain, liver etc[span_334](start_span)[span_334](end_span).
  • Other Artifacts During Autopsy: Extravasation of blood can occur. Hyoid bone fracture due to rough handling[span_335](start_span)[span_335](end_span).
  • Toxicological Artifacts (During Autopsy): Contamination of viscera with stomach contents. Faulty technique of sample collection gives false results[span_336](start_span)[span_336](end_span).
Importance of Knowledge of PM Artifacts in Improving Standard of Medicolegal Autopsy:
Misinterpretation may lead to wrong cause and manner of death and miscarriage of justice[span_337](start_span)[span_337](end_span).
