Post-Mortem Protocols

01. Core Concepts & Definitions

AUTOPSY: A systematic post-mortem examination of a dead body to determine the cause of death, identify disease or injury, and study pathological changes.
NEGATIVE AUTOPSY: A post-mortem examination in which no definite cause of death can be determined, even after a complete and thorough autopsy, including histopathology, toxicology, and microbiology.

Primary Objectives

  • Establish positive identification of the deceased.
  • Determine the cause, manner (natural, accidental, suicidal, homicidal), and time since death.
  • Demonstrate, measure, and record all external and internal structural malformations or traumatic defects.
  • Collect viscera and fluid samples for histological, toxicological, and microbiological screening.
  • In newborns: determine proof of live birth and viability.

03. Legal Framework & Rules (Nepal)

  • Authorization: Conducted strictly under official written requisition from the police or Chief District Officer (CDO).
  • Location: Performed only in authorized government institutions/mortuaries. Never in private rooms.
  • Practitioner: Executed by a registered medical practitioner trained in forensic medicine.
  • Daylight Rule: Examination must preferably be performed in natural daylight. If body arrives at night, conduct a preliminary surface exam (rigor, lividity) and defer internal cuts until dawn.
  • Data Integrity: Erasures or overwriting in the protocol are illegal. Corrections must be struck through with a single line and initialed.
  • Report Policy: The post-mortem protocol is a confidential legal document submitted straight to investigating authorities; it is never handed over directly to relatives.

04. Surgical Incisions (Viva Essentials)

I-Shaped Incision

Runs from the chin down the midline to the symphysis pubis. Crucial adjustment: Deviates to the left or right around the umbilicus to bypass its dense, fibrous structure, which compromises secure post-exam suturing. Used for standard cases.

Y-Shaped Incision

Begins at both acromion processes, sweeping under the breasts to meet at the xiphoid process before continuing down the midline to the pubis. Selected when chest wall cosmetic preservation is required for viewing or custom arrangements.

Modified Y-Shaped Incision

Commences at the suprasternal notch, runs down to the pubis, with lateral incisions extending behind both ears over the neck. Mandatory for hanging and strangulation cases to afford bloodless dissection exposure of delicate neck cartilage and vascular structures.

05. Internal Visceral Protocols

Cardiovascular Dissection

  1. Assess the pericardial sac in situ for volumetric fluid shifts (hemopericardium/tamponade, purulent exudate).
  2. Open chambers strictly in the direction of systemic blood flow: Right Atrium → Right Ventricle → Pulmonary Artery → Left Atrium → Left Ventricle → Aorta.
  3. Document ventricular wall dimensions (checking for left-sided concentric hypertrophy) and map coronary artery cross-sections for atherosclerotic blocks.
Viva Differentiation Point:
• Antemortem Thrombus: Firm, friable, dry, stuck tightly to endothelial walls (Lines of Zahn).
• Postmortem Clot: Elastic, non-adherent, glistening, showing classic "chicken-fat" upper layers and heavy sedimented "currant-jelly" bases.

Cranial Cavity Protocol

  1. Run a coronal incision from ear to ear across the vertex. Reflect the scalp anteriorly over the supraorbital ridges and posteriorly past the occipital protuberance.
  2. Saw the skull vault along a circular track clear of the supraorbital rims. Lift the calvarium flap to scrutinize the dura mater for signs of extra/subdural hematomas.
  3. Sever cranial nerve roots cleanly from front to back, ease the brain out gently, and slice coronally through cerebral tissue to visualize intraventricular or basal ganglia bleeds.

06. Applied Forensic Scenarios (Exam Case Studies)

Case A: Obstructed Labor (Maternal & Fetal Demise)

Clinical Scenario: A 22-year-old female presenting with full-term obstructed labor; both mother and child found dead.

MATERNAL FINDINGS (Mother)
• External: Signs of severe exhaustion/dehydration, dry mucous membranes, marked vulval and vaginal edema, bruising/lacerations of the birth canal. Bandl’s ring (pathological retraction ring) may be visible across the lower abdomen.
• Internal: Uterine rupture (usually lower segment) with extensive intraperitoneal hemorrhage, or massive ischemic necrosis of the uterine wall. The fetal head may be wedged tightly into the pelvic brim.
• Cause of Death: Hemorrhagic shock (secondary to uterine rupture) OR Septic shock (secondary to prolonged infection/chorioamnionitis).
• Manner of Death: Natural.
• Mechanism of Death: Acute cardiovascular collapse / Multiorgan dysfunction syndrome (MODS).
FETAL FINDINGS (Baby)
• External: Extreme distortion and moulding of the skull bones. Massive caput succedaneum or extensive cephalhematoma. Peeling skin or maceration changes depending on time elapsed since intrauterine death.
• Internal: Intracranial hemorrhage (subdural/subarachnoid) secondary to severe bone overriding. Asphyxial signs: widespread visceral petechiae (Tardieu spots on pleura/pericardium), thick meconium aspiration in the trachea and major bronchi.

Case B: Pulmonary Findings in Drowning Autopsy

A classic, comprehensive autopsy of a lung from a body retrieved from water reveals the following distinct forensic features:

  • Emphysema Aquosum: The lungs are massively voluminous, heavy, and overdistended, meeting in the midline and completely hiding the pericardium. They retain their shape and do not collapse when lifted from the thoracic cavity.
  • Edema Aquosum: The lungs feel boggy and doughy, pitting on pressure. Slicing through the parenchyma reveals immense fluid congestion; a profuse mix of watery fluid and fine, blood-tinged froth exudes heavily from the cut surfaces.
  • Paltauf’s Hemorrhages: Subpleural ecchymoses (pale, irregular, faint hemorrhages) are clearly visible beneath the visceral pleura, particularly along the margins of the lower lobes, caused by the rupture of alveolar walls.
  • Airway Intrusion: The trachea, main bronchi, and deep alveolar networks are filled with a tenacious, fine, persistent froth that doesn't disappear on wiping, often accompanied by aspirated foreign particles (silt, sand, weeds, or diatoms).
