Terminology Frameworks

  • Infanticide: Unlawful destruction of a child under the age of one year. committed at the time of, or within a few minutes or hours after, birth. It excludes death during labour via craniotomy or decapitation.
  • Foeticide: Killing of the fetus at any time prior to birth.
  • Filicide: The deliberate act of killing of a child by the parent (from Latin filius: son).
  • Neonaticide: Deliberate killing of a child within 4 weeks of its birth.

Still Birth vs. Dead Birth

Still Birth

A still born fetus is born after 28 weeks of pregnancy (24 weeks in UK) and shows no sign of life after complete birth. The child was alive in utero but dies during birth.

  • Risk Factors: More common in illegitimate, immature male children of primipara mothers.
  • Signs of Prolonged Labour: Edema and bleeding into the scalp, caput succedaneum, and severe moulding of the head.
  • Causes: Prematurity, anoxia, birth trauma (intracranial hemorrhage from moulding), placental abnormalities, toxemia of pregnancy, erythroblastosis fetalis, and congenital defects.

Crucial Viva Signatures: Squames or meconium within alveoli do NOT indicate still birth; meconium staining of the body merely flags fetal distress. Medico-legally, a charge of infanticide will fail if the baby is proven stillborn.

Dead Birth

A dead born child has died entirely in utero and presents specific postmortem features immediately upon delivery.

  • Signs of Dead Birth: Rigor mortis at delivery, maceration, and putrefaction.

Age Estimation & Fetal Metrics

  • Up to 5 Months: $\sqrt{\text{Height in cm}} = \text{Age in months}$
  • After 5 Months: $\text{Length in cm} / 5 = \text{Age in months}$
  • Ossification Centres: At 7 months, ossification centres appear on the lower end of the femur (thigh bone) and upper part of the tibia. Also present in foot bones and sternum. Ossification centres provide more reliable evidence of maturity than length alone.

Live Birth & Establishment of Respiration

Live birth requires the child to show signs of life when even only a part of the child is out of the mother, regardless of whether it has breathed yet or is completely born. Causing its death is legally categorized as homicide/infanticide.

Anatomical Status: Before vs. After Respiration

Shape of Chest Flat; circumference is 1-2 cm less than abdomen at umbilicus level. Expands and becomes arched or drum-shaped.
Diaphragm Position Level at 4th or 5th rib. Level at 6th or 7th rib.
Lung Position Lies at the back of the thoracic cavity. Volume increases, fills the thoracic cavity.
Lung Volume & Margin Small, collapsed onto hilum; sharp margins. Voluminous, fills pleural cavity; round margins.
Consistency & Color Dense, firm, liver-like, non-crepitant; uniform reddish-brown/violet. Inflated, spongy, elastic, crepitant; mottled pink or marbled.
Squeezing under Water Any gas bubbles present escape. Air bubbles expelled as a stream of minute dots rising to surface.
Blood Bed & Weight Half the blood volume of respired lung; weighs 30-40 gm. Twice the blood volume of unrespired lung; weighs 60-66 gm.
Plocquet's Ratio Lung weight to body weight is about 1:70. Lung weight to body weight is about 1:35.

Hydrostatic Test Whole lungs and separated parts sink in water. Floats in water.
Microscopic Structure Not expanded; sac closed, lined with cuboidal cells. Expanded; sac dilated, lined by squamous cells.
Vagitus Phenomena:
• Vagitus Vaginalis: Child cries with its head inside the vagina.
• Vagitus Uterinus: Child cries inside the uterus due to air entering the uterine cavity.
Proof of breathing (crying) is not absolute legal proof of live birth since a child can die of natural causes before being completely born. Law presumes a child was born dead until postmortem examination proves otherwise.

Postmortem Evidence of Separate Existence

  • Breslau's Second Life Test (Stomach-Bowel Test): Air swallowed during breathing causes the stomach and intestines to float when removed under double ligatures; they sink if unrespired. Presence of milk provides positive proof of live birth.
  • Changes in Blood & Skin: Nucleated RBCs disappear within 24 hours. Fetal Hb drops from 80-90% rapidly. Vernix caseosa coats joint flexures. Skin turns bright red at birth, darker at 2-3 days, and normal by 1 week.
  • Meconium Clearance: Large intestine becomes completely free of meconium within 24 hours after a live birth. (Delayed or passed in utero in breech/hypoxia cases).
  • Wredin's Test: Gelatinous embryonic tissue in the middle ear is replaced by air upon respiration as the Eustachian tube sphincter relaxes.

The Hydrostatic Test

Principle: Based on specific gravity shifts. Unrespired lung specific gravity sits at 1040–1050 (sinks); post-respiration, it drops to 940 due to tidal air entry, causing it to float in water.

Procedural Cascade

Tie ligatures on bronchi → Separate lungs → Place individual intact lungs in water
↓
If lungs sink → Cut into 12–20 pieces → Test pieces in water
↓
If pieces sink → Squeeze pieces between fingers underwater to watch for escaping air dots
↓
Wrap pieces in cloth → Apply pressure weight to expel decomposition gases & check for residual flotation

Fallacies & Diagnostic Exceptions

  • False Positives (Unrespired Lungs Float): Putrefaction (decomposition gases) or artificial inflation via forceful mouth-to-mouth resuscitation.
  • False Negatives (Respired Lungs Sink): Very feeble/short respiration, atelectasis, pulmonary edema, or congenital pneumonitis.
Hydrostatic Test is Unnecessary If:
1. Fetus exhibits gross fatal congenital malformations (e.g., anencephaly/monster).
2. Fetus is under 28 weeks gestational age (immature).
3. Stomach contains milk or umbilical cord shows cicatrization/healing.
4. Fetus shows signs of intra-uterine maceration or mummification.
5. Bruises are evident on lung surfaces from active respiratory effort.

Maturity & Causes of Infant Death

Appearance of Full-Term Mature Infant

  • Metrics: Crown-heel length: 48-52 cm; Crown-rump length: 28-32 cm; Weight: 2500-3300 gm (females average 100 gm less).
  • Features: Lanugo restricted to shoulders; head hair 2-3 cm long. Nails project beyond fingertips; nose/ear cartilage formed. Testicles descended into scrotum or labia majora covering the vulva.

Criminal Modes of Infanticide

  • Acts of Commission: Positive acts causing death.
    • Suffocation: Smothering with pillows/hands (requires minimal force, leaves no trauma). Forcing mud, rags, or cotton wool into the airway.
    • Strangulation: Ligature, hands, or simulating natural cord twisting around the neck.
    • Pithing (Concealed Puncture): Piercing the anterior fontanelle with a fine needle to damage brain structures; child dies later of induced meningitis mimicking natural infection.
    • Trauma: Head crushing, throwing against walls, fractures/dislocations of cervical vertebrae.
  • Acts of Omission (Neglect): Failure to provide proper assistance during labor, failure to tie the cut cord (death from hemorrhage), exposure to elements, or withholding food/clearance of airways.
Precipitate Labour Forensic Defense:
Precipitate labour terminates suddenly and rapidly without warning, merging all three stages of labour. Legally, it acts as a critical cross-examination point: mothers accused of infanticide may claim the child died accidentally due to head injury from dropping onto a hard floor, or suffocated in a lavatory pan during sudden bowel transit.

Child Abuse & Battered Baby Syndrome

Battered Baby Syndrome (Caffey's / Maltreatment Syndrome) describes non-accidental violence inflicted repeatedly on a child (usually under 3 years old) by a custodian adult. It features a sharp discrepancy between physical findings and the history provided.

Diagnostic and Physical Stigmata

  • Skin and Facial Markings: Bruises of varying ages and healing stages. Gripping the chest forcefully leaves clustered coin-shaped bruises along the ribs called "six penny" bruises. Pinching creates butterfly-shaped lesions. Blows to the mouth often tear the upper lip frenulum against the teeth.
  • Skeletal Traumas (X-Ray Highlights): Multiple fractures across different anatomical sites. Frequent periosteal hematomas form because infant periosteum strips easily. Violent shaking triggers posterior rib fractures close to the spine; as callus develops over 1–2 weeks, they form "nobbing fractures" appearing on X-rays like a string of beads.
  • Infantile Whiplash Syndrome: Violent shaking causes rotational acceleration forces, rupturing delicate bridging intracranial vessels to form subdural hematomas, accompanied by intraocular and retinal hemorrhages.
  • Visceral & Other Injuries: Abdominal trauma causing liver laceration/hemoperitoneum, clean-cut small bowel injuries, circular pitted cigarette burns, and traumatic alopecia (clump hair pulling).

Munchausen & Munchausen Syndrome by Proxy

  • Munchausen Syndrome: An adult compulsively and consciously feigns, exaggerates, or self-induces illness to undergo unnecessary hospital investigations and treatments.
  • Munchausen Syndrome by Proxy (MSBP): A dangerous form of child abuse where a parent (typically the mother) fabricates or actively induces illness in her child to secure medical attention and satisfy a psychological need for sympathy.

Rosenberg Diagnostic Criteria

  1. Illness produced, fabricated, or alleged entirely by a parent or guardian.
  2. Repeated requests for medical care resulting in multiple invasive, unnecessary procedures.
  3. Parental denial of any knowledge regarding the true etiology of the symptoms.
  4. Complete regression or disappearance of symptoms when the child is separated from the parent.
Common Simulation Methods: Mixing maternal pricked blood into the child's urine, inducing suffocation/apnea with pillows, administering insulin to induce hypoglycemia, using ipecac to mimic cyclic vomiting, laxatives for diarrhea, or dosing with theophylline to trigger aerobics.

Sudden Infant Death Syndrome (SIDS)

SIDS (Cot / Crib Death) is the sudden and unexpected death of a seemingly healthy infant which remains completely unexplained even after a thorough autopsy, death scene investigation, and clinical history review.

Epidemiological Profile

  • Age & Sex: Occurs from 2 weeks to 2 years, peaking between 2 and 4 months, with a slight male predominance.
  • Risk Enhancers: Sleep periods, twins, prematurity, low socioeconomic status, maternal gestational smoking, or maternal drug abuse.
  • Proposed Pathologies: Prolonged sleep apnea cascades, localized upper respiratory cow-milk hypersensitivity, conduction anomalies, or medullary/hippocampal immunoreactivity.

Autopsy Presentations (Diagnosis of Exclusion)

  1. External Findings: Milk- or blood-stained froth at the mouth, nostrils, or bedding. Infant's hands are frequently clenched tightly around bedcloth fibers. Petechiae may spot the face and eyes.
  2. Internal Findings: Trachea containing milk vomit with shed epithelial cells. Prominent petechial hemorrhages over the lung surfaces, thymus, and posterior epicardial surface of the heart. Lungs are heavy, congested, firm, and exhibit patchy uniform purplish discoloration with alveolar collapse.
