Classification Framework

Thermal injuries encompass structural and systemic trauma resulting from pathologically extreme deviations in environmental or applied temperature.

  • Exposure to Cold:
    • General effects: Hypothermia.
    • Local effects: Trench foot, Frostbite, and Pernio (Chilblains).
  • Exposure to Heat:
    • General effects: Heat cramps, Heat exhaustion, and Heat stroke.
    • Local effects: Thermal Burns and Scalds.

Cold Injury: Systemic & Local Pathology

1. Hypothermia ("White Death")

  • Definition: Systemic medical condition where core body temperature drops below 35°C (95°F) due to cold exposure.
  • Severity Staging:
    • Mild: 32–35°C
    • Moderate: 28–32°C
    • Severe: < 28°C
  • Risk Profile: High vulnerability in infants, individuals over 60, mountaineers, sailors, and patients with hypothyroidism, dementia, or acute intoxication (alcohol, opiates).
  • Pathological Effects: Direct cell damage is concentrated in fatty tissues and myelinated nerve fibers. Indirect systemic injuries stem from severe tissue ischemia.
  • Complications: Hemorrhagic pancreatitis, acute tubular necrosis, focal myocardial fiber necrosis, pneumonia, and gastrointestinal ulceration.

Autopsy Signatures of Hypothermia

  1. External Signatures:
    • Pinkish Postmortem Lividity: Caused by the high persistence of oxyhemoglobin in the superficial capillaries under low temperatures.
    • Pale/White Skin: Gives rise to the forensic term "white deaths". Red erythematous patches may be localized over joints and bony prominences.
    • Rigor Mortis: Characterized by a markedly slow onset and extended postmortem duration.
  2. Internal Signatures:
    • Wischnewski Ulcers: Small, dark, mucosal hemorrhages and erosions clustering across the gastric mucosa.
    • Respiratory System: Highly edematous lungs with fresh intraparenchymal hemorrhages; the trachea contains fine froth indicative of terminal bronchospasm.
Key Forensic Behaviors in Hypothermia:
• Paradoxical (Reciprocal) Undressing: Occurs due to cold-induced paralysis of the hypothalamic heat-regulatory center. Smooth muscle in the cutaneous arterioles fails to maintain vasoconstriction, triggering a sudden rush of warm blood from the core to the periphery. This creates an intense, false sensation of extreme warmth, causing the confused victim to remove their clothing before death.
• Hide and Die Syndrome: An instinctive protective "burrowing" or "cave-like" behavior driven by terminal mental confusion and hibernation reflexes. Victims crawl into cramped, strange spaces (under beds, behind wardrobes) or pull household items over themselves.

2. Immersion Syndrome (Trench Foot)

  • Etiology: Prolonged, continuous exposure to severe, non-freezing cold (< 10°C) combined with moisture and dampness. Historically common in trench warfare or maritime emergencies.
  • Clinical Progression Stages:
    1. Pre-hyperemic stage: Affected extremities are pale, cold, and anesthetic.
    2. Hyperemic stage: Extremities turn hot, accompanied by intense burning and shooting pain.
    3. Post-hyperemic stage: The area turns pale or cyanotic with diminished arterial pulsations.
  • Management: Air drying and gradual rewarming by exposure to ambient air at room temperature. Contraindications: Direct application of heat, ice, water immersion, or aggressive physical massage.

3. Frostbite (Congelatio)

  • Etiology: Direct tissue injury from freezing conditions (≤ -2.5°C), resulting in the formation of ice crystals within cells and mechanical obstruction of local microcirculation.
  • Presentation: Distinct blue-black discoloration localized to peripheral, exposed tissues (fingers, toes, nose, ears, face).
  • Staging Depth:
    • Mild: Restricted to the epidermis and subcutaneous layers; symptoms include numbness, prickling, and localized itching.
    • Deep: Extends into deep fascial structures and muscles, producing severe paresthesia, tissue stiffness, and a yellow-white, immobile, inelastic appearance of the skin.
  • Microscopy: Severe endothelial cell destruction, serum leakage into the interstitial space, and intravascular sludging of red blood cells.
Absolute Vital Phenomenon:
Frostbite requires active, functional systemic circulation and inflammatory responses to develop its characteristic line of demarcation. Therefore, frostbite can only be produced during life and cannot develop postmortem.

Presents with Postmortem Caloricity (the body temperature remains elevated for an extended period after death). Features include rapid onset of rigor mortis, rapid putrefaction, sinking of the eyeballs, cerebral edema, and widespread petechial hemorrhages throughout the cerebral white matter and internal viscera.

Thermal Burns: Core Pathology

Burns are injuries caused by the application of thermal energy, radiation, or chemical substances to body surfaces, leading to tissue destruction.

Etiological Types

  1. Contact Burns: Direct physical contact with a hot solid or liquid metal. Produces erythema and blistering that matches the size and shape of the object.
  2. Flame Burns: Direct contact with open fire. Characterized by blistering, singeing of hair, and blackening/charring of the skin.
  3. Radiant Heat Burns: Caused by electromagnetic heat waves without direct contact. Skin changes from erythematous to light brown and leathery.
  4. Chemical Burns: Caused by corrosives (acids/alkalis). Characterized by ulcerated patches; blisters, singed hair, and red lines of demarcation are absent.

Surface Area Estimation: Wallace's Rule of Nine (Adults)

  • Head and Neck: 9%
  • Each Upper Extremity: 9%
  • Anterior Torso: 18%
  • Posterior Torso: 18%
  • Each Lower Extremity: 18%
  • Perineum/Genitals: 1%
  • The patient's palm represents 1% of Total Body Surface Area (TBSA) for small burn estimations.
Pediatric Correction (Lund and Browder):
In infants under 1 year, the head is relatively large, representing 18% of TBSA, while each leg is reduced to 14%. For each year of age above 1, add 0.5% to each leg and subtract 1.0% from the head until adult ratios are reached.

Causes of Death in Burn Trauma

  • Immediate Pathways: Primary neurogenic shock from severe pain; acute suffocation from inhaling carbon monoxide (CO), carbon dioxide (CO2), or hydrogen cyanide; or fatal respiratory/vagal cardiac arrest triggered by heat-induced laryngospasm.
  • Delayed Pathways: Hypovolemic burn shock; acute edema of the glottis from inhaling toxic smoke; acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS); toxemia; or systemic sepsis (the single most common delayed cause of death).
  • Remote Pathways: Chronic multi-organ failure from prolonged suppurative infection; severe anemia; or fatal gastrointestinal hemorrhage from Curling's ulcers (stress-induced mucosal ischemia in the gastric antrum or duodenum developing after 72 hours).

Autopsy Findings in Charred/Burnt Bodies

External Pathology Signatures

  • Crow's Feet Pattern: Unburnt, pale skin creases radiating from the corners of the eyes. This is a vital reaction indicating that the victim was alive and squinting their eyes tightly to shield them from smoke irritation.
  • Cherry-Red Staining: Postmortem lividity appears bright cherry-red due to high levels of carboxyhemoglobin (CO-Hb) in the blood, proving the victim breathed in fire gases while alive.
  • Pugilistic Attitude (Fencing/Boxing Stance): A universal posture characterized by flexion of the hips, knees, and elbows, with the fingers curled like claws. This is an artifact caused by the thermal coagulation of muscle proteins and tissue dehydration. Because flexor muscles have larger mass than extensors, their contraction dominates, pulling the limbs into flexion. This occurs regardless of whether the person was alive or dead when exposed to the fire.
  • Heat Ruptures: Large splits in the skin over fleshy, burnt areas (thighs, calves) caused by thermal tissue contraction. They mimic incised or lacerated wounds but can be identified by intact nerves and blood vessels bridging the split, an absence of tissue bruising, and a lack of clotted blood within the fissure.
  • Hair Changes: Hair becomes fragile, curly, and clubbed at the tips because the keratin shaft melts and resolidifies. Gray hair turns a reddish-brown color, while black hair remains black.

Internal Artifacts & Pathognomonic Signs

  • Heat Hematoma: A postmortem artifact that closely mimics a true extradural hematoma. It forms a large, thick (up to 1.5 cm) chocolate-colored clot of 100–120 ml over the dura. It occurs as thermal energy forces blood marrow out of the diploic veins of the calvarium into the extradural space.
  • Soot Deposits: Inhaled carbon and soot particles trapped within the mucus of the larynx, trachea, and bronchioles provide definitive proof of antemortem exposure to fire.

Scalds & Chemical Burn Modifiers

Scalds are injuries resulting from the application of steam or liquids heated above 60°C. Unlike dry burns, scalds do not cause charring or singeing of hair, and tissue damage rarely extends below the dermis.
